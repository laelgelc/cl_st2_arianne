Factor f1 (positive pole) is best interpreted as a “Greenpeace movement memory and identity” dimension, centred on human-authored, historically grounded, person‑centred narrative about the organisation and its wider ecology/peace milieu.

Below I unpack the evidence and then propose concise labels.

---

### 1. Which group drives this pole?

Mean scores:

- Human: 24.69 (very high)
- persona_gpt: 12.11 (moderate)
- persona_grok: 4.57 (low)
- persona_gemini: 3.99 (low)

So the positive pole is overwhelmingly driven by **human-authored texts**. LLM persona texts sit much closer to zero, suggesting they only partially reproduce the discourse style encoded here.

The high‑scoring examples are all human texts, and they are strongly biographical, historical, and reflective.

### 2. Lexical profile of the pole

The highest loadings are very revealing:

- **People and roles**: warrior, son, writer, father, daughter, officer, teacher, man, mother, woman, activist, ecologist, environmentalist, journalist, artist, lawyer, director, manager, president, captain, student, volunteer, citizen, politician, parent, colleague, friend, member, person, someone, us, humanity.
- **Movement / organisation / media**: media, editor, book, film, university, school, union, committee, organisation, organization, board, campaign, movement, group, alliance, team, communications, communication, news, website, platform, twitter, post.
- **Narrative and reflection**: story, history, experience, memory, background, belief, idea, opinion, thought, feeling, passion, inspiration, dream, vision, spirit, sense, understanding, relationship.
- **Temporal and life‑course**: birth, age, morning, night, spring, day, year, week, month, summer, century, beginning.
- **Conflict / peace / activism frame**: war, peace, bomb, weapon, attack, battle, warrior, revolution, police, officer, cancer (often metaphorical or biographical), demonstration.
- **Greenpeace‑specific ecology lexis**: ecology, ecologist, environmentalist, earth, whale, radiation, port, coast, island, lake, village, park, world, society.

This is not a technical climate-science vocabulary; it is a **social, biographical, organisational, and ideological lexicon**. It foregrounds:

- named and role‑defined people
- their relationships and life histories
- the organisational infrastructure of Greenpeace and allied movements
- reflective, evaluative language about ideas, beliefs, and strategies.

---

### 3. What the high‑scoring texts actually do

Across the excerpts, the same discourse pattern recurs. Key features:

#### a) Biographical and commemorative storytelling

Many texts are extended biographies or collective memoirs:

- Life of Bob Hunter, his philosophy, spiritual influences, media theory, burnout, death, and legacy (f1_pos_001, 004, 006, 008).
- Irving and Dorothy Stowe’s life trajectory, political decisions, migration, family, and role in founding Greenpeace (f1_pos_003).
- Founders’ round‑table recollections of the first voyage (f1_pos_002).
- Profiles of volunteers and activists (f1_pos_005).
- Sketches of key women in ecological thought and Greenpeace history (f1_pos_010).

These are **thick, human‑centred narratives**: childhood, family, marriages, jobs, illnesses, deaths, spiritual journeys, and personal turning points.

#### b) Movement genealogy and institutional memory

The texts repeatedly reconstruct:

- How Greenpeace emerged from the **peace movement, Quaker non‑violence, civil rights, feminism, anti‑Vietnam activism**.
- The formation of specific committees, offices, campaigns, and international structures (e.g. Don’t Make a Wave Committee, Greenpeace International, early offices).
- The role of **media tactics** (“mind bombs”, benefit concerts, iconic photographs, radio rooms, press calls).

This is **movement self‑history**: how we began, who we were, what we did, how we learned.

#### c) Interweaving ecology with culture, politics, and spirituality

The discourse constantly links ecology to:

- peace and anti‑nuclear politics (bomb, war, peace, weapons testing)
- feminism and gender (Betty Friedan, ecofeminism, women’s roles)
- spirituality (Taoism, Buddhism, Indigenous teachings, “Mother Earth”)
- media and culture (Marshall McLuhan, Joni Mitchell, Jerry Garcia, novels, poetry).

So ecology is framed as a **civilisational and cultural shift**, not just environmental policy.

#### d) Reflexive strategic thinking

Several texts are meta‑strategic:

- Debates about non‑violence vs. revolution, communications vs. armed struggle.
- Critiques of climate conferences, calls to “flip the script”, boycott, invent new tactics (f1_pos_007).
- Reflections on organisational creativity, bureaucracy, movement vs. organisation.
- Honest accounts of internal conflict, power struggles, leadership dilemmas (f1_pos_006).

This is **self‑critical, reflective movement discourse**: how we think about what we do.

#### e) Emphasis on relationships and affect

The narratives foreground:

- friendships, mentorships, surrogate parent–child bonds (e.g. Cormack as father figure to Hunter).
- grief, burnout, trauma, love, humour, hope, passion, determination.
- the idea that “behind the science … there are real people with real stories” (f1_pos_005).

So the discourse is **relational and emotional**, not just informational.

### 4. How the loading words show up in the examples

Many of the top loadings are directly instantiated:

- warrior, Rainbow Warriors, bomb, war, peace, weapon, officer, police, battle, attack – in narratives of anti‑nuclear and anti‑war activism.
- writer, journalist, editor, book, author, media, film, photographer – in accounts of columns, books, photos, and media strategy.
- father, mother, son, daughter, family, child, parent – in biographical sketches and intergenerational stories.
- activist, ecologist, environmentalist, lawyer, teacher, student, union, president, manager – as roles within the movement and its allies.
- story, history, movement, campaign, group, organisation, board, committee – in recounting campaigns and organisational development.
- belief, idea, opinion, passion, spirit, vision, dream, hope, fear – in articulating philosophies and emotional states.

The lexical pattern and the discourse function line up: this pole is where the corpus “remembers itself” as a human movement.

---

### 5. Why LLM persona texts score lower

Persona‑prompted LLM texts, even when mimicking Greenpeace, tend to:

- focus more on **issue framing, calls to action, and generic advocacy**;
- use less dense **named‑person biography, organisational minutiae, and long historical narrative**;
- be less candid about internal conflict, failure, and ambivalence.

So they partially pick up the activist and value‑laden vocabulary, but not the **deep, archival, memory‑work** that characterises these human texts. That’s consistent with the mean scores.

---

### 6. Candidate labels and justification

Any label should capture:

- human‑authored, narrative, reflective style
- focus on people, roles, relationships, and life stories
- reconstruction of Greenpeace’s origins, culture, and strategy
- integration of ecology with peace, feminism, spirituality, and media.

Possible labels:

1. **“Greenpeace movement memory and identity discourse”**  
   - “movement memory” captures the historical, commemorative, genealogical work.  
   - “identity” captures the construction of who “we” are: founders, volunteers, allies, values, tactics.  
   - Fits the heavy use of story, history, biography, and organisational detail.

2. **“Biographical–historical ecology activism discourse”**  
   - Highlights the biographical and historical narrative mode.  
   - Anchors it in ecology activism rather than generic biography.

3. **“Narrative eco‑movement self‑reflection”**  
   - Emphasises the reflexive, strategic, and philosophical tone.  
   - “Self‑reflection” points to the meta‑discussion of tactics, failures, and organisational form.

Of these, I would prioritise:

- **Primary label**:  
  **Greenpeace movement memory and identity discourse**

- **Secondary descriptive gloss**:  
  A biographical–historical, people‑centred narrative mode in which Greenpeace and allied eco‑peace actors recount their origins, relationships, philosophies, and strategic lessons.

This captures both the lexical pattern and the discourse functions evidenced in the high‑scoring texts.
