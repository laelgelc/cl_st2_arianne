## 1. Possible labels for f2 (neg) pole

I would propose labels along the lines of:

1. **“Consumer‑goods and retail activism discourse”**  
2. **“Everyday consumption and brand‑focused campaigning”**  
3. **“Lifestyle commodities and corporate accountability discourse”**  

All three capture the same core: texts that frame environmental issues through consumer products, retail spaces, and named brands/companies, often in the context of campaigns and regulations.

## 4. Evidence from the example excerpts

Although the excerpts are only a sample, they align well with the loadings.

### 4.1 Fashion and clothing consumption

Excerpts 001–003 (Gemini persona) are classic **consumer‑lifestyle campaign texts**:

- Lexical matches: fashion, clothes, brands.  
- Content:  
  - Clothes swap parties, fast fashion, Detox campaign, eco‑fashion, interactive maps (Excerpt 001).  
  - Personal narrative of not buying new clothes, thrift‑shopping, upcycling, Black Friday, “wear it 30 times” (Excerpt 002).  
  - Progress of fashion brands on Detox commitments, ranking companies (H&M, Zara, Benetton, Nike, Victoria’s Secret) (Excerpt 003).

These texts:

- Focus on **everyday consumer choices** (buying, swapping, repairing clothes).  
- Name **specific brands and campaigns** (Detox, Adidas, Puma, Nike, H&M, Zara).  
- Frame environmental harm via **lifestyle commodities** (fast fashion, disposable fashion).

This is exactly what a “consumer‑goods and retail activism” dimension would capture.

### 4.2 Plastics, bags, bottles, supermarkets

Excerpts 005 and 009:

- Lexical matches: bag, bottle.  
- Content:  
  - Single‑use plastic bags, microplastics, “Reduce, Reuse, Recycle,” beach cleans (Excerpt 005).  
  - Global bans on plastic bags and bottles; specific jurisdictions (France, Morocco, Karnataka, San Francisco, Honolulu, Tasmania, Ethiopia); links to ocean pollution and a Greenpeace pledge (Excerpt 009).

Again, the discourse is:

- **Highly consumer‑facing** (bags, bottles, cosmetics with microbeads).  
- **Retail and policy oriented** (bans, regulations, enforcement, seizures, pledges).  
- Tied to **ocean wildlife** (turtles, whales, seabirds) and **beaches**, which match the loading set (turtle, beach, seafood).

### 4.3 Retailers, supermarkets, and food safety

Excerpt 008 (no explicit loading word in the snippet, but consistent with the set):

- Mentions **supermarkets**, a **retailer (Lianhua)**, pesticide tests on produce, plastic‑wrapped items, and a **city‑wide traceability system**.  
- Connects **consumer food choices** and **retail supply chains** to environmental and health outcomes.

This fits the **retail / supermarket / customer** cluster in the loadings.

### 4.4 Greenpeace identity and iconic campaigns

Some loadings (captain, warrior, rainbow, shell, seal, station, arctic, amazon) point to:

- **Greenpeace’s own brand and history** (Rainbow Warrior, “warriors of the rainbow,” ship captains, Arctic Sunrise, etc.).  
- **Iconic campaign sites and targets** (Arctic drilling, Amazon deforestation, Shell, seal hunts).

Excerpt 006 (Bob Hunter, “Warriors of the Rainbow”) illustrates this identity‑building discourse, even though the loading words themselves are not highlighted in the snippet.

So, the negative pole also encodes **Greenpeace’s self‑presentation as a campaigning organization**, often in relation to high‑profile corporate and geopolitical actors.

## 5. Integrating loadings and examples: what discourse is encoded?

Putting the evidence together:

- **The loadings** show a strong concentration of:
  - Consumer and retail vocabulary (fashion, clothes, bags, bottles, supermarkets, brands, customers, packaging, sale).  
  - Named campaign arenas and actors (Arctic, Amazon, Shell, UN, ministries, republics, Rainbow Warrior, seals, turtles, beaches, seafood).  

- **The examples** show:
  - Campaign narratives that **translate global environmental issues into everyday consumption practices** (what you wear, what you buy, how you shop, which bags you use).  
  - A focus on **corporate accountability and policy change** (Detox commitments, bans on plastics, lawsuits against retailers, traceability systems, brand rankings).  
  - Frequent **calls to individual action** framed through consumer roles (swap clothes, refuse bags, avoid microbeads, join pledges, set personal shopping goals).

Crucially, this is not a purely technical or scientific climate discourse; it is **campaign‑oriented, brand‑named, and lifestyle‑mediated**. Environmental harm is narrated through:

- **Products** (clothes, bags, bottles, seafood).  
- **Places of purchase** (supermarkets, retailers).  
- **Corporate and governmental actors** (brands, Shell, ministries, UN, republics).  
- **Charismatic species and iconic regions** (seals, turtles, Arctic, Amazon, beaches).

This justifies interpreting the f2 negative pole as a **lexical dimension of environmental discourse that foregrounds consumer goods, retail spaces, and branded campaigns as the main interface between the public and climate/eco issues**.

## 6. Why these labels fit

1. **“Consumer‑goods and retail activism discourse”**  
   - “Consumer‑goods” captures fashion, clothes, bags, bottles, packaging, seafood.  
   - “Retail” captures supermarkets, retailers, customers, sales.  
   - “Activism discourse” captures the campaigning, bans, lawsuits, rankings, pledges, and Greenpeace’s own identity (Rainbow Warrior, Shell campaigns, Arctic/Amazon actions).

2. **“Everyday consumption and brand‑focused campaigning”**  
   - “Everyday consumption” reflects the lifestyle framing (what you wear, what you buy, how often you use it).  
   - “Brand‑focused” reflects the naming and ranking of companies and retailers (H&M, Zara, Nike, Lianhua, Shell, Thai Union, Walmart, Sainsbury’s).  
   - “Campaigning” reflects the strong presence of bans, lawsuits, pledges, and Greenpeace actions.

3. **“Lifestyle commodities and corporate accountability discourse”**  
   - “Lifestyle commodities” foregrounds fashion, fast fashion, eco‑fashion, plastic bags, bottles, cosmetics.  
   - “Corporate accountability” foregrounds the pressure on brands, retailers, and corporations, often in conjunction with ministries, UN, and republics.

Any of these labels would be an appropriate, corpus‑grounded characterization of the **negative pole of Factor 2** in this LMDA.
